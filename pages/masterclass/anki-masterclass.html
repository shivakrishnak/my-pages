Why It Works

The Cognitive Science Behind Retention

Anki is not magic — it is the implementation of two of the most replicated findings in cognitive psychology. Understanding why these work transforms how you design cards.

The Testing Effect (Retrieval Practice)

Roediger & Karpicke (2006) showed that students who studied by retrieval testing recalled 50% more material one week later than those who studied by re-reading. The act of attempting to retrieve a memory — even failing — strengthens the memory trace far more than passive review.

Key insight
The struggle of retrieval IS the learning. Effortless recall means the card is too easy. Difficulty is a signal, not a failure.

The Spacing Effect

Reviewing information at increasing intervals — just as you are about to forget it — causes dramatically deeper re-encoding than massed review. Ebbinghaus (1885) first documented the forgetting curve. Cepeda et al. (2008) confirmed optimal spacing: review when retention drops to ~70%, not when it drops to 0%.

Why Anki works
Anki's SM-2 algorithm calculates the optimal next review interval based on your rating. Your only job is card quality — the algorithm handles when.

Desirable Difficulties

Bjork's research shows that conditions making learning feel harder in the moment — interleaving, retrieval practice, spacing — produce dramatically better long-term retention. This is why Anki cards often feel harder than just reading notes. The difficulty is the mechanism, not a flaw.

Elaborative Interrogation

Cards that ask "Why does this happen?" produce stronger retention than cards that ask "What is this?" The brain encodes meaning into a network of prior knowledge. The more connections, the more retrieval paths.

"Memory is not a single thing; it is a web of associations. Every connection you build is another path to retrieval."

— Robert Bjork, UCLA Memory & Cognitive Control Laboratory
Foundations

The 5 Laws of Permanent Recall

Every card you create should pass these laws. A card that violates any one of them will either be forgotten quickly, cause confusion during review, or waste your review time.

01
Minimum Information
One atomic idea per card. If the answer requires "and" or "also" — split the card. Compound answers prevent clean retrieval.
02
Test Understanding, Not Memory
Cards that ask you to copy a definition test recognition, not understanding. Test application, consequence, or explanation instead.
03
Context on the Front
The cue should trigger real-world thinking. Replace "What is X?" with "You're facing [situation]. How does X apply?"
04
Multiple Angles
For anything important, create 3–5 cards from different directions. A concept you can only recall one way is fragile.
05
Ruthless Filtering
Most information does not deserve a card. Ask: "Will I be embarrassed not to know this in a year?" If unsure, don't card it yet.
∞
Understand First
Never card something you don't understand. Confusion encoded is confusion reinforced. Card knowledge, not confusion.
Card Quality × Consistency of Review
= Permanent Recall
Both factors are necessary. Great cards reviewed once a year will be forgotten. Mediocre cards reviewed daily will be misunderstood forever.

Design principle
For every important concept, create at least one card at each Bloom level. Start from the bottom but weight your deck toward the top. "Remember" cards are the cheapest to create and the least valuable.
Workflow

The Card Creation Protocol

Follow this protocol every time you finish learning something new. It takes 5–10 minutes and produces cards that survive years of review.

1
Filter ruthlessly
Before opening Anki, ask: "Will I need this in one year? Would I be embarrassed not to know this?" If unsure, skip. Most notes don't deserve cards. The goal is a small, high-signal deck, not a mirror of your notes.
2
Understand before encoding
Write the core idea in one sentence. If you can't compress it, you don't understand it yet. Confusion encoded is confusion reinforced. Read, discuss, ask Claude, draw it out — then card it.
3
Create the atomic Basic card
Write one Q&A that captures the essential fact with a consequence. Not "What is X?" — instead "What does X enable that Y doesn't?" Constrain the question so the answer is predictable.
4
Add an Application card
Write a scenario where you'd need to use this concept. The front describes a situation, the back walks through the reasoning. This is the most valuable card type — don't skip it.
5
Add a Cloze from a key sentence
Find the single sentence that best encodes this concept. Delete the most important 1–3 words. Each deletion becomes a separate card automatically in Anki.
6
Add Explain Simply for core concepts
For anything foundational, create a card that asks you to explain it to a 12-year-old using a fresh analogy. You cannot fake this one. Rate yourself strictly: "Could a non-expert follow this?"
7
Self-review once before adding
Read the front. Can you predict the back? If yes — too easy, make the front harder. If you have no idea — too vague, constrain the question or add context. Then add to Anki.
What Not To Do

Common Anti-Patterns

These are the card mistakes that cause decks to decay, review sessions to drag, and knowledge to remain shallow. Each has a direct fix.

✗ Anti-Pattern 1: Copy-paste definitions
Q: What is Big O notation?
A: Big O notation is a mathematical notation that describes the limiting behavior of a function when the argument tends towards a particular value or infinity.
Tests recognition of a sentence. Doesn't test if you can use or reason with the concept. You'll "remember" this but not understand it.
✓ Fixed: Consequence-focused
Q: You have O(n²) code and 10× more data. What happens to runtime — and why does Big O matter for this decision?
A: Runtime grows ~100×. Big O describes growth rate, not raw speed — so a 10× data increase squares the work for O(n²) but barely changes O(log n).
Tests real reasoning. You have to understand growth rates, not recall a definition.
✗ Anti-Pattern 2: Compound questions
Q: What are the four ACID properties and what does each mean?
A: Atomicity (all-or-nothing), Consistency (valid state), Isolation (concurrent txns don't interfere), Durability (committed = permanent).
Four facts in one card. You'll almost always remember some but not all, creating ambiguous ratings and weak encoding for each.
✓ Fixed: One fact per card (×4)
Q: A payment succeeds but the inventory update fails. Which ACID property determines whether both operations are treated as one unit?
A: Atomicity — the transaction is all-or-nothing. Both succeed or both roll back. No partial states.
One crisp fact, tested with a real scenario. Make four cards like this, one per property.
✗ Anti-Pattern 3: No context on the front
Q: What is the CAP theorem?
A: A distributed system can only guarantee two of three: Consistency, Availability, Partition Tolerance.
"What is X" tests nothing. You'll remember the acronym but not when or how to use it.
✓ Fixed: Situation-based
Q: Your distributed DB is partitioned. Nodes can't sync. The CAP theorem says you must choose — what's the choice and what does each option cost?
A: Consistency vs Availability. Choose C: reject writes until partition heals (safe, unavailable). Choose A: accept writes that may conflict (available, inconsistent).
Tests that you understand the real trade-off, not just the acronym expansion.
✗ Anti-Pattern 4: No reverse direction
Q: What is a monad in functional programming?
A: A design pattern wrapping a value with context (Maybe, Promise, Result) that allows chaining operations without explicit null-checking.
You can recall the definition when given the name. But can you recognize a monad when you see one? Probably not.
✓ Fixed: Add a reverse recognition card
Q: You see code that chains .flatMap() on a nullable type to avoid null checks at each step, wrapping the result at the end. What pattern is this?
A: A monad (specifically a Maybe/Option monad). The flatMap chains operations on the wrapped value, short-circuiting on null/None automatically.
Recognition in context is a different skill from definition recall. Both cards together = robust understanding.
Ruthless Filtering

What Not to Memorize

The most underrated Anki skill is knowing what to exclude. A bloated deck with low-signal cards destroys motivation and dilutes review quality. Apply these filters before creating any card.

Memorize this
  • Core principles that apply across many situations
  • Distinctions that are easy to confuse (mutex vs semaphore)
  • Order-of-magnitude facts (O(log n) vs O(n))
  • Mental models you'll reach for daily
  • Vocabulary that unlocks reading in a domain
  • Counterintuitive results that change behavior
  • Clinical thresholds / diagnostic criteria in medicine
  • Syntax you use weekly but keep forgetting
Don't card this
  • Anything you can look up in 10 seconds
  • Things you use so often you can't forget them
  • Information you've never needed once in practice
  • Details inside details (version numbers, minor APIs)
  • Things you don't understand yet
  • Trivia with no application to your actual work or goals
  • Content from a book chapter you skimmed once
  • Anything where you can't write a real scenario for it
The 1-year test
Before creating a card, ask: "If I forgot this for one year, would it cost me something real?" If the answer is no — skip it. If yes — card it and make sure the card tests use, not recognition.
Advanced Technique

Cloze Deletion Mastery

Cloze cards are the fastest to create and — when designed well — among the most powerful. Most people use them badly. Here is the advanced approach.

The Context Rule

The deleted word must be deducible from context — not random. The surrounding sentence should make the deleted term feel inevitable in retrospect. If you could delete any word and the card would still make sense, you're doing it wrong.

// Bad — no context, could be anything Q: {{c1::Redis}} is a database. // Good — context forces the specific term Q: When you need sub-millisecond latency for session storage and can tolerate data loss on crash, you choose {{c1::Redis}} over PostgreSQL because it keeps the working set {{c2::in memory}}. // Two deletions = two cards. Each tests different knowledge. A1: Redis (when: sub-ms latency + tolerable data loss) A2: In memory (why Redis is fast)

Sentence Selection

Choose sentences that encode a mechanism or consequence, not just a description. The best cloze sentences contain: a subject, a condition, and an outcome. "When [condition], [X] causes [outcome]."

Multiple Deletions from One Sentence

Anki treats each {{c1::}}, {{c2::}} etc. as a separate card. A single well-crafted sentence can generate 3–5 cards that each test a different dimension of the same fact. This is extremely high ROI.

// Single sentence → 4 cards TCP guarantees {{c1::ordered}} delivery via {{c2::sequence numbers}}, at the cost of {{c3::latency}}, making it unsuitable for {{c4::real-time applications like gaming}}. c1: ordered c2: sequence numbers c3: latency c4: real-time applications like gaming

Domain-Specific Card Strategy

Different fields have different knowledge shapes. Use this guide to choose the right card type for each domain.

Biology / Medicine Image Occlusion Cloze Mechanisms over names: "What causes [symptom] at the molecular level?" Long lists of symptoms without mechanistic grouping
Mathematics Basic Explain Simply "Give an intuitive reason why the derivative of sin(x) is cos(x)" over memorizing the derivative table Memorizing formulas without knowing what they mean geometrically

Long-term

Rating Your Cards Correctly

Honest rating is the single highest-leverage Anki skill. Most users rate too generously, which inflates intervals and causes cards to be shown when they're actually forgotten.

Rating When to use it Effect
Again (1) You forgot it completely, or remembered it wrong Resets to learning phase. Interval back to minutes.
Hard (2) You got it but it took significant effort or hesitation Interval multiplied by ~1.2
Good (3) You recalled correctly with minor effort Interval multiplied by ease factor (~2.5)
Easy (4) You recalled instantly with zero effort Large interval jump. Consider making the card harder.
Golden rule
If "Easy" comes up more than 20% of the time across your deck, your cards are too simple. Rebuild them at a higher Bloom level.
Habit Design

Daily Workflow

A world-class card deck is useless without a sustainable daily practice. Design the system for your worst day, not your best.

The Minimum Viable Day

Define your MVD for Anki: 10 minutes of reviews, no new cards. This is the non-negotiable minimum that preserves your work. On hard days, do only this. The streak lives. The deck stays current.

System design insight
New cards are optional. Reviews are mandatory. Reviews prevent decay. New cards increase load. When overwhelmed, stop adding cards — never stop reviewing.

The Daily Flow

☀
Morning review (10–20 min)
Review due cards first thing. Do not check your phone, email, or news first. Fresh cognitive resources for retrieval practice. This is the highest ROI time for review.
📖
Learn, filter, card (during study)
When you finish a chapter, article, or learning session — spend 5 min filtering and creating cards. Don't batch it to the end of the week. Memory fades fast; card while the context is warm.
🌙
Evening quality check (weekly)
Once per week, browse flagged/leeched cards. A leech (failed 8+ times) is a design problem, not a memory problem. Redesign the card, not your brain.

Managing Leech Cards

A leech is a card you keep failing. This is never a memory failure — it is always a card design failure. For every leech, ask:

  • Is it two facts disguised as one? Split it.
  • Is the front too vague? Add context, scenario, or constraint.
  • Do I not actually understand the concept? Study first. Delete the card. Rebuild after understanding.
  • Is the answer too long to reliably produce? Shorten to the core insight.
  • Is it something I never actually use? Delete it without guilt.
Mastery

The Review Ritual

How you review matters as much as whether you review. These practices turn Anki from passive repetition into active mastery.

Active Recall Mode

Before flipping the card, say the answer out loud (or write it). The act of production — not just recognition — is what drives the testing effect. If you can only vaguely recall the answer, rate it Again or Hard, even if you were technically correct.

The Generative Variation

When reviewing an application card, before reading your pre-written answer — try to generate a different example than the one on the card. This trains flexible application rather than memorized answer playback.

Interleaving

Anki's algorithm naturally interleaves topics. Resist the urge to review only one deck at a time. The context-switching between topics (physics card, then biology card, then CS card) feels harder — and produces 40–50% better long-term retention (Rohrer & Taylor, 2007).

"The feeling of not knowing is the signal that learning is happening. The feeling of ease is the signal that you're in your comfort zone."

— Robert Bjork, on desirable difficulties in learning

Pre-Review Priming

Before beginning a review session, spend 60 seconds mentally activating the domains you'll be reviewing. Think: "What did I learn about distributed systems last week? What was confusing about TCP?" This pre-activation primes related memory networks and improves retrieval success during the session.
