Why It Works

The Cognitive Science Behind Retention

Anki is not magic — it is a precise implementation of two of the most replicated findings in cognitive psychology. Understanding why these mechanisms work transforms how you design every card.

The Testing Effect

Roediger & Karpicke (2006) showed that students who studied by retrieval testing recalled 50% more material one week later than those who studied by re-reading the same content for the same amount of time. The act of attempting to retrieve a memory — even failing — strengthens the memory trace far more than passive review. Failure during retrieval is productive, not a setback.

Core mechanism The struggle of retrieval IS the learning. Effortless recall means the card is too easy. Difficulty is a signal of growth, not of failure.

The Spacing Effect

Reviewing information at increasing intervals — just as you are about to forget it — causes dramatically deeper re-encoding than massed review. Ebbinghaus (1885) first documented the forgetting curve. Cepeda et al. (2008) confirmed the optimal spacing principle: review when retention drops to ~70%, not when it reaches 0%. Anki's SM-2 algorithm handles this automatically. Your only job is card quality.

Desirable Difficulties

Bjork's research demonstrates that conditions making learning feel harder in the moment — retrieval practice, spacing, interleaving — produce dramatically better long-term retention. Anki sessions often feel harder than reading your notes. That difficulty is the mechanism, not a flaw in the system.

Elaborative Interrogation

Cards that ask "Why does this happen?" produce stronger retention than cards asking "What is this?" The brain encodes meaning into a network of prior knowledge. Every connection you build is another retrieval path. Isolated facts have one path in and one path out. Networked understanding has dozens.

"Memory is not a single thing; it is a web of associations. Every connection you build is another path to retrieval." — Robert Bjork, UCLA Memory & Cognitive Control Laboratory
First Principles

The 5 Laws of Permanent Recall

Every card you create should pass all five laws. A card that violates even one will be forgotten quickly, cause confusion during review, or waste irreplaceable review time.

01
Minimum Information
One atomic idea per card. If the answer requires "and" or "also" — split the card. Compound answers prevent clean retrieval and make honest rating impossible.
02
Test Understanding, Not Memory
Cards that ask you to copy a definition test recognition, not understanding. Always test application, consequence, comparison, or explanation instead.
03
Context on the Front
The cue should trigger real-world thinking. Replace "What is X?" with "You're facing [situation]. How does X apply?" The situation is the retrieval cue you'll actually encounter.
04
Multiple Angles
For anything important, create 3–5 cards from different directions. A concept you can only recall one way is fragile. Robust knowledge is a lattice, not a chain.
05
Ruthless Filtering
Most information does not deserve a card. Ask: "Would I be embarrassed not to know this in a year?" A bloated deck dilutes every review session.
∞
Understand Before Encoding
Never card something you don't understand. Confusion encoded is confusion reinforced. Study first, clarify fully — then create the card.
Card Quality × Review Consistency = Permanent Recall
Both factors are necessary. Great cards reviewed once a year will be forgotten. Mediocre cards reviewed daily will be misunderstood forever.
Card Architecture

The 6-Type Card System

Different types of knowledge require different card shapes. Using only one type produces a fragile, one-dimensional understanding. Master all six, deploy them by situation.

Type Best for Bloom level Primary risk
Basic Q&A Atomic facts, definitions with consequence Remember Too vague → recognition, not recall
Cloze Deletion Key terms inside context sentences Understand Over-using → becomes pattern-matching
Application Principles, frameworks, mental models Apply / Analyze Overlong answers → skipped during review
Forward + Reverse Vocabulary, formulas, bidirectional concepts Understand Forgetting to create the reverse direction
Explain Simply Complex mechanisms — the Feynman test Evaluate / Create Rating yourself too generously
Image Occlusion Diagrams, anatomy, circuits, spatial systems Apply Too many occlusions per image = overwhelm
Design principle For every important concept, create at least one card at each Bloom level. Weight your deck toward the top three levels. "Remember" cards are cheapest to make and least valuable to keep.

Basic Q&A
Workflow

The Card Creation Protocol

Follow this protocol every time you finish learning something new. It takes 5–10 minutes per concept and produces cards that survive years of review without becoming leech cards.

  1. Filter ruthlessly. Ask: "Will I need this in one year? Would I be embarrassed not to know this?" If unsure — skip it. Most notes don't deserve cards. The goal is a small, high-signal deck, not a mirror of your notes.
  2. Understand before encoding. Write the core idea in one sentence. If you can't compress it, you don't understand it yet. Confusion encoded is confusion reinforced. Study, discuss, draw it out — then card it.
  3. Create the atomic Basic card. Write one Q&A that captures the essential fact with its consequence. Not "What is X?" — instead "What does X enable that Y doesn't?" Constrain the question so the answer is predictable.
  4. Add an Application card. Write a scenario where you'd need to use this concept. The front describes a situation, the back walks through the reasoning. This is the most valuable card type — don't skip it for anything you care about.
  5. Add a Cloze from a key sentence. Find the single sentence that best encodes this concept. Delete the most important 1–3 words. Each deletion becomes a separate card automatically in Anki.
  6. Add Explain Simply for foundational concepts. Create a card that asks you to explain it using a fresh analogy — to a 12-year-old, not a peer. Rate yourself strictly: "Could a non-expert follow this?"
  7. Self-review once before adding. Read the front. Can you predict the back? If yes — too easy, make the front harder. No idea at all — too vague, add context or constraint. Then add to Anki.
Card Mistakes

Anti-Patterns & Fixes

These are the card mistakes that cause decks to decay, review sessions to drag, and knowledge to stay shallow. Each has a precise fix.

Anti-Pattern 1 — Copy-paste definitions

✗ Bad card
Q: What is Big O notation?
A: Big O notation is a mathematical notation that describes the limiting behavior of a function when the argument tends towards a particular value or infinity.
Tests recognition of a sentence you read. You'll "remember" this but never be able to use it under any real condition.
✓ Fixed
Q: Your code runs in O(n²). You get 10× more data. What happens to runtime — and why does Big O matter for this decision?
A: Runtime grows ~100×. Big O describes growth rate, not raw speed. A 10× data increase squares the work for O(n²) but barely changes O(log n).
Tests real reasoning. You must understand growth rates, not recall a definition.

Anti-Pattern 2 — Compound questions

✗ Bad card
Q: What are the four ACID properties and what does each mean?
A: Atomicity (all-or-nothing), Consistency (valid state), Isolation (no interference), Durability (committed = permanent).
Four facts in one card. You'll almost always remember some but not all, producing ambiguous ratings and weak encoding for each.
✓ Fixed — one card per property
Q: A payment succeeds but the inventory update fails. Which ACID property determines whether both operations are treated as one unit?
A: Atomicity — the transaction is all-or-nothing. Both operations succeed or both roll back. No partial states exist in the system.
One crisp fact tested with a real scenario. Create four cards like this, one per ACID property.

Anti-Pattern 3 — No context on the front

✗ Bad card
Q: What is the CAP theorem?
A: A distributed system can only guarantee two of three: Consistency, Availability, Partition Tolerance.
"What is X" tests nothing. You'll recall the acronym expansion but not when or how to apply it under real conditions.
✓ Fixed — situation-based
Q: Your distributed DB is partitioned. Nodes can't sync. The CAP theorem says you must choose — what's the choice, and what does each option cost the user?
A: Consistency vs Availability. Choose C: reject writes until partition heals (safe, unavailable). Choose A: accept writes that may conflict (available, eventually inconsistent).
Tests real trade-off reasoning. The scenario IS the real-world trigger for this knowledge.

Anti-Pattern 4 — Missing reverse direction

✗ Bad card — one direction only
Q: What is a monad in functional programming?
A: A design pattern wrapping a value with context (Maybe, Promise, Result) allowing chaining without explicit null-checking.
You can recall the definition when given the name. But can you recognize a monad when you encounter one in unfamiliar code? Almost certainly not.
✓ Fixed — add reverse recognition
Q: You see code that chains .flatMap() on a nullable type, short-circuiting on null at each step and wrapping the final result. What pattern is this?
A: A monad (specifically a Maybe/Option monad). The flatMap chains operations on the wrapped value, automatically skipping on null/None without explicit null checks.
Recognition in context is a completely different skill from definition recall. You need both cards.
Ruthless Filtering

What Not to Memorize

The most underrated Anki skill is knowing what to exclude. A bloated deck destroys motivation and dilutes review quality. Apply these filters before creating any card.

Do memorize this
  • Core principles that apply across many situations
  • Distinctions that are easy to confuse under pressure
  • Order-of-magnitude facts (O(log n) vs O(n²))
  • Mental models you'll reach for daily
  • Vocabulary that unlocks reading an entire domain
  • Counterintuitive results that change real behavior
  • Diagnostic criteria, clinical thresholds in medicine
  • Syntax you use weekly but keep looking up
Don't card this
  • Anything you can look up in under 10 seconds
  • Things you use so often you can't forget them
  • Information you've never needed once in practice
  • Version numbers, minor API signatures
  • Things you don't understand yet — study first
  • Trivia with no application to your actual goals
  • Content from a chapter you skimmed once
  • Anything you can't write a real scenario for
The 1-year test Before creating a card, ask: "If I forgot this for one year, would it cost me something real?" If the answer is no — skip it without guilt. If yes — card it, and make sure the card tests use, not recognition.
Advanced Technique

Cloze Deletion Mastery

Cloze cards are the fastest to create and — when designed well — among the most powerful. Most people use them badly. Here is the production-level approach.

The Context Rule

The deleted word must be deducible from context — not arbitrary. The surrounding sentence should make the deleted term feel inevitable in retrospect. If you could delete any word and the card would still make sense, you're doing it wrong.

// ✗ Bad — no context, answer could be anything Q: {{c1::Redis}} is a database. // ✓ Good — context forces the specific answer Q: When you need sub-millisecond latency for session storage and can tolerate data loss on crash, you choose {{c1::Redis}} over PostgreSQL because it keeps the working set {{c2::in memory}}. // Two deletions = two separate Anki cards. A1: Redis (when: sub-ms latency + tolerable data loss) A2: in memory (why Redis is fast)

Sentence Selection

Choose sentences that encode a mechanism or consequence, not just a description. The best cloze sentences contain: a subject, a condition, and an outcome. Structure: "When [condition], [X] causes [outcome]."

Multiple Deletions from One Sentence

Anki treats each {{c1::}}, {{c2::}} as a separate card. A single well-crafted sentence can generate 3–5 cards, each testing a different dimension of the same fact. This is extremely high ROI.

// Single sentence → 4 Anki cards automatically TCP guarantees {{c1::ordered}} delivery via {{c2::sequence numbers}}, at the cost of {{c3::latency}}, making it unsuitable for {{c4::real-time applications like gaming}}. c1: ordered c2: sequence numbers c3: latency c4: real-time applications like gaming
Advanced: Hint cloze Add a hint inside the deletion: {{c1::Redis::in-memory store}}. The hint appears when you're reviewing so you know which concept to probe for — without giving away the answer itself. Useful for complex technical terms.

Long-term

Rating Your Cards Honestly

Honest rating is the highest-leverage Anki skill. Most users rate too generously, inflating intervals and causing cards to appear when they're actually forgotten.

Rating When to use it Interval effect
Again (1) Forgot it completely, or remembered it wrong Resets to learning phase. Interval back to minutes.
Hard (2) Got it but it took significant effort or hesitation Interval multiplied by ~1.2
Good (3) Recalled correctly with minor effort — the target state Interval multiplied by ease factor (~2.5×)
Easy (4) Recalled instantly with zero effort Large interval jump. Consider making the card harder.
Golden rule If "Easy" comes up more than 20% of the time in your deck, your cards are too simple. Rebuild them at a higher Bloom level. Easy cards are wasted review time.
Habit Design

Daily Workflow

A world-class deck is worthless without a sustainable practice. Design the system for your worst day, not your best day. Consistency matters more than intensity.

The Minimum Viable Day

Define your MVD for Anki: 10 minutes of reviews, zero new cards. This is the non-negotiable minimum that preserves your existing work. On hard days, do only this. The streak survives. The deck stays current.

System principle New cards are optional. Reviews are mandatory. New cards increase your load. Reviews prevent decay. When overwhelmed — stop adding cards. Never stop reviewing.

Managing Leech Cards

A leech is a card you keep failing (Anki default: 8 failures). This is never a memory failure — it is always a card design failure. For every leech, ask:

  • Is it two facts disguised as one? Split it into separate cards.
  • Is the front too vague? Add a scenario, constraint, or context.
  • Do I not actually understand the concept? Study first. Delete. Rebuild after understanding.
  • Is the answer too long to reliably produce? Shorten to the single core insight.
  • Is it something I never use in practice? Delete it without guilt. Ruthless filtering applies retroactively.
Mastery

The Review Ritual

How you review matters as much as whether you review. These practices turn Anki from passive repetition into active mastery.

Active Recall Mode
Before flipping any card, say the answer out loud — or write it. The act of production, not recognition, drives the testing effect. If you can only vaguely recall the answer, rate it Again or Hard even if you were technically correct.
Generative Variation
When reviewing an application card, generate a different example than the one on the card before reading your pre-written answer. This trains flexible application rather than memorized answer playback.
Embrace Interleaving
Anki's algorithm naturally interleaves topics. Resist the urge to review one deck at a time. The context-switching between domains feels harder and produces 40–50% better long-term retention (Rohrer & Taylor, 2007).
Pre-Session Priming
Spend 60 seconds before reviewing mentally activating the domains ahead. Think: "What did I learn about distributed systems last week?" Pre-activation primes related memory networks and improves retrieval success during the session.
"The feeling of not knowing is the signal that learning is happening. The feeling of ease is the signal that you're in your comfort zone." — Robert Bjork, on desirable difficulties in learning
The 4 principles to take away
Card quality first
No algorithm compensates for poorly designed cards. Invest in card quality before reviewing quantity.
Filter ruthlessly
A deck of 200 excellent cards beats a deck of 2,000 mediocre ones. Less is permanently more.
Rate honestly
Generous ratings are self-deception. Honest ratings are the only signal the algorithm has. Guard them carefully.
Show up daily
10 consistent minutes beats 90 sporadic minutes. The forgetting curve does not wait for convenient review sessions.
