Your Weekly Plan

Built for general fitness, endurance, and strength — not bodybuilding. Five sessions a week, 45–60 minutes each, using the standard equipment available at Cult Neo Gym Mallapur (barbells, dumbbells, cable/functional trainer, smith machine or squat rack, leg press, lat pulldown, chest press, treadmill/cross-trainer).

This is an Upper/Lower split: two lower-body days, two upper-body days, and one dedicated cardio/conditioning day, with two rest days. Every session opens with a hip-hinge or core-stability move chosen specifically because of your lower back sensitivity — the goal is to build a strong, pain-resistant back, not just avoid aggravating it.

Why This Split
Five compound-lift-based sessions a week, alternating upper and lower body, give each muscle group roughly 48 hours to recover between sessions while still training everything twice a week — the rep range proven most reliable for building strength and muscle endurance together, per ACSM/NSCA general fitness guidelines.
Why So Few Exercises
5–6 exercises per day, all compound (multi-joint) movements. Research consistently shows a handful of well-executed compound lifts builds more real-world strength and fitness than 15 isolation exercises — and it's far easier to recover from and stay consistent with.
First time at the gym, or returning after a break? Open the How-To Library tab now and skim through it before your first session. You don't need to memorize anything — just get a rough sense of what each movement looks like so Monday doesn't feel like learning twenty new things at once.

Saturday & Sunday — Rest / Active Recovery

Rest days are when your muscles actually repair and grow stronger — they're not optional, they're part of the plan.

What To Do
A relaxed 30–40 minute walk on one or both days. Light stretching, especially hips, hamstrings, and lower back. Playing with your kids counts as activity — no need to "do more." Prioritize sleep — this is where most muscle recovery happens.
What To Avoid
Heavy lifting or high-intensity training. Sitting completely still all day if avoidable — gentle movement helps back pain more than total rest. Skipping both rest days even if you feel good — recovery is when adaptation happens.

Principles & Progression Rules

The rules that make this plan work over months, not just one week.

Progressive Overload — How To Add Weight
The 2-for-2 rule: If you complete all prescribed sets and reps with good form for 2 consecutive sessions on an exercise, add a small amount of weight next time — typically 2.5kg for upper body lifts and 2.5–5kg for lower body lifts. If form breaks down or you miss reps, stay at the same weight until you complete it cleanly.
Starting Weights
The weight numbers in this plan are sensible starting points for someone with some training experience, not fixed targets. Your first 1–2 weeks should feel like a "could do a few more reps" effort — this lets you learn each movement pattern safely before adding real load.
Tracking Progress
Keep a simple note on your phone: date, exercise, weight, reps completed. Five minutes a week of tracking is what turns "going to the gym" into measurable, lasting strength and fitness gains.
Rest Between Sets
The rest periods listed (45–90 seconds) are based on the goal of each exercise: compound lower-body lifts need more recovery (75–90s) than isolation or core work (45s). Resting properly is part of the program, not wasted time.
Nutrition & Sleep Basics
No exercise program outperforms poor sleep or poor eating. Aim for 7+ hours of sleep, adequate protein (roughly 1.2–1.6g per kg bodyweight daily, so ~85–110g for you), and consistent meals.
Back Pain — Specific Guidance For You
Your back pain pattern (slight, linked to carrying toddlers and long sitting) is extremely common and responds very well to exactly the kind of training in this plan: hip hinge strength (Romanian deadlifts, hip thrusts), anti-extension core work (planks, dead bugs, bird dogs), and glute strengthening. These rebuild the support structure around your spine.
Always Do
Brace your core (like before a light punch to the stomach) before any lift involving your back. Keep your lower back's natural curve — neither rounded nor over-arched — during squats and hinges. Stop a rep immediately if you feel sharp or shooting pain (different from normal muscle fatigue).
Avoid For Now
Heavy barbell deadlifts from the floor until your hip-hinge pattern is confident. Loaded spinal flexion exercises like weighted sit-ups or heavy Russian twists. Lifting your kids by bending your back — bend your knees and hips instead, same pattern you're training on Mondays.

1 Goblet Squat Legs ›
QuadsGlutes CoreInner thighs

A squat where you hold one dumbbell vertically against your chest. The weight in front naturally keeps your torso upright, which is why this is the easiest, safest squat variation to learn — and exactly why it opens Monday's session.

Setup

Stand with feet shoulder-width apart, toes turned out slightly (about 15°). Hold one dumbbell vertically, cupping the top end with both hands, and pull it into your chest like holding a goblet.

How to do it — step by step
  1. Take a breath, brace your stomach as if about to be tapped there.
  2. Push your hips back and bend your knees together, lowering straight down — imagine sitting into a chair behind you.
  3. Keep the dumbbell close to your chest the whole way down; let your elbows slide between your knees as you get low.
  4. Go down until your thighs are roughly parallel to the floor, or as low as you can while keeping your heels flat and back straight.
  5. Drive through your whole foot (not just your toes) to stand back up, squeezing your glutes at the top.
  6. That's one rep. Breathe out as you stand.
What it should feel like

Effort in your thighs and glutes. Your knees should track in the same direction as your toes — not caving inward. Your heels stay planted on the floor throughout; if they lift, you've gone lower than your current ankle mobility allows — that's fine, just don't force it deeper.

Common mistakes
  • Rounding the lower back at the bottom — stop the descent earlier if this happens
  • Knees collapsing inward — actively push them out in the direction of your toes
  • Rising onto toes — keep weight through the whole foot, especially the heel
2 Romanian Deadlift (Dumbbell) LegsBack-friendly ›
HamstringsGlutesLower back (stabilizing)

Not a "deadlift off the floor" — this version starts standing and only travels down to about shin height. It's a hip-hinge: your hips move backward, not your knees bending forward. This is the single most important movement pattern for protecting your lower back during everyday life, like lifting your kids.

Setup

Stand holding a dumbbell in each hand in front of your thighs, palms facing your body. Feet hip-width apart, soft (not locked) knees.

How to do it — step by step
  1. Brace your core. Keep a very slight bend in your knees and keep it constant — it doesn't change throughout the movement.
  2. Push your hips straight back, like closing a car door with your butt. Your chest stays up, back stays flat.
  3. Let the dumbbells slide straight down along the front of your legs as your hips travel back.
  4. Go down until you feel a clear stretch in your hamstrings — for most beginners this is around shin or mid-shin height, not the floor.
  5. Reverse the motion by driving your hips forward to standing, squeezing your glutes hard at the top.
What it should feel like

A stretching sensation in the back of your thighs (hamstrings) as you lower, and a squeeze in your glutes as you stand. You should not feel this in your lower back — if you do, you're hinging too far or rounding your spine.

Common mistakes
  • Squatting instead of hinging — your knees should barely bend more than their starting position
  • Rounding the back to reach lower — stop the descent the instant you feel your back start to curl
  • Letting the dumbbells drift away from your legs — keep them brushing against your thighs/shins
Start lighter than feels necessary. This exercise's value comes from the controlled hip-hinge pattern, not the weight. Master the motion with light dumbbells (or even just your hands) for the first week before adding real load.
3 Leg Press (Machine) LegsBack-friendly ›
QuadsGlutesHamstrings

A seated machine that lets you push a weighted platform away with your legs. The seat back fully supports your spine, making this one of the safest ways to build serious leg strength regardless of back history.

How to do it — step by step
  1. Sit in the machine with your back and head flat against the pad. Place feet shoulder-width apart on the platform, roughly in the middle.
  2. Release the safety handles and let the platform lower under control until your knees reach about a 90° bend.
  3. Press through your whole foot to push the platform back up.
  4. Stop just short of locking your knees out completely at the top — keep a soft bend.
What it should feel like

Pure leg effort — your back stays passively supported by the pad the entire time. If you feel your lower back lifting off the pad at the bottom of the movement, you've gone too low for your current flexibility — stop a little higher.

Common mistakes
  • Locking knees out hard at the top — keeps tension off the joint, stay soft
  • Letting knees cave inward as you push — track them over your toes
  • Lowering so far that your hips/back lift off the pad — find your comfortable range
4 Standing Calf Raise Isolation ›
Calves

Standing on flat ground (or the edge of a small step for extra range), you rise up onto your toes and lower back down. Simple, but builds real ankle and calf endurance that helps with walking, running, and everyday stamina.

How to do it — step by step
  1. Stand tall, holding a dumbbell in each hand if adding weight (optional for the first weeks).
  2. Rise up onto the balls of your feet as high as you can, pause for 1 second at the top.
  3. Lower back down slowly, under control — don't just drop.

If using a step: let your heels dip slightly below the step's edge at the bottom for a deeper stretch.

5 Hip Thrust (Barbell or Machine) LegsBack-friendly ›
GlutesHamstrings

Arguably the single best exercise for building strong glutes — and strong glutes are your spine's best natural protector during everyday lifting. Your upper back rests on a bench while you drive a weighted bar (or machine pad) upward with your hips.

Setup

Sit on the floor with your upper back against the edge of a bench. Roll a barbell (with a pad across it for comfort) or position the machine pad over your hips. Feet flat on the floor, knees bent.

How to do it — step by step
  1. Brace your core and dig your heels into the floor.
  2. Drive your hips straight up by squeezing your glutes — not by arching your lower back.
  3. At the top, your body should form a straight line from your shoulders through your hips to your knees.
  4. Pause and squeeze your glutes hard for 1 second at the top.
  5. Lower back down under control until your hips are just above the floor, then repeat.
Common mistakes
  • Overarching the lower back at the top instead of squeezing glutes — the line should be straight, not banana-shaped
  • Pushing through toes instead of heels — heels do the work here
  • Letting the bar/pad roll away from your hip crease — keep it positioned right at the hip joint
6 Bulgarian Split Squat Legs ›
QuadsGlutesBalance

A single-leg squat with your rear foot elevated behind you on a bench. Harder to balance than a regular squat at first, but excellent for building leg strength evenly on both sides and improving stability.

Setup

Stand a couple of feet in front of a flat bench, facing away from it. Place the top of one foot on the bench behind you (laces down). Most of your weight should be on your front leg.

How to do it — step by step
  1. Keep your torso upright and core braced.
  2. Lower straight down by bending your front knee, until your front thigh is close to parallel with the floor.
  3. Your back knee will dip toward the floor but shouldn't touch it.
  4. Push through your front foot to stand back up.
  5. Complete all reps on one leg before switching sides.
If balance is hard at first

Use the easier variation instead: a static split squat with both feet on the ground (one foot forward, one foot back, both touching the floor). Same up-and-down motion, much more stable. Progress to the rear-foot-elevated version once that feels comfortable.

7 Leg Curl (Machine) Isolation ›
Hamstrings

A machine (lying or seated, whichever your gym has) where you curl a padded lever using just your hamstrings. Directly strengthens the back of your thighs, which balances out all the quad-dominant work from squats and lunges, and supports your lower back.

How to do it — step by step
  1. Position yourself per the machine — lying face down or seated, with the pad resting just above your ankles.
  2. Curl the pad toward your glutes (lying version) or down toward the floor (seated version) in a slow, controlled motion.
  3. Pause briefly at full contraction.
  4. Lower back to the start slowly — don't let the weight stack drop.
8 Hip Abduction (Cable or Machine) Isolation ›
Outer glutes/hips

Strengthens the muscles on the outside of your hips, which stabilize your pelvis when walking and running — a commonly neglected area that, when weak, contributes to knee and lower-back strain.

How to do it — step by step
  1. On a seated machine: sit with the pads against the outside of your knees/thighs, push your legs apart against the resistance, then return slowly.
  2. On a cable: attach an ankle strap, stand side-on to the machine holding something for balance, and lift the working leg out to the side, then return slowly.
9 Walking Lunge Warm-up ›
QuadsGlutesBalance

Used here as a warm-up — bodyweight only, no dumbbells needed. Step forward into a lunge, alternating legs as you move across the floor.

How to do it — step by step
  1. Step forward with one leg, landing on your heel first.
  2. Lower your back knee toward the floor until both knees are around 90°.
  3. Push off your front foot to step through into the next lunge with the opposite leg.
  4. Continue alternating legs as you move forward.
10 Chest Press (Flat or Incline, Machine) PushBack-friendly ›
ChestShouldersTriceps

A seated machine that supports your back fully while you press handles away from your chest. The flat version targets the chest evenly; the incline version (seat angled up) shifts more emphasis to the upper chest and shoulders.

How to do it — step by step
  1. Sit with your back flat against the pad, handles roughly at chest height.
  2. Grip the handles, brace your core.
  3. Press the handles forward until your arms are extended but not locked.
  4. Control the weight back to the starting position — don't let it slam.

The machine keeps your back supported throughout, which is exactly why it's the recommended choice over a barbell bench press for now.

11 Seated Shoulder Press PushBack-friendly ›
ShouldersTriceps

Pressing weight straight overhead while seated, with back support — either a machine or dumbbells while sitting on a bench with a back pad. Seated with support is much easier on your lower back than standing overhead presses.

How to do it — step by step
  1. Sit with your back against the pad, holding the handles (or dumbbells) at shoulder height, elbows bent.
  2. Press straight upward until your arms are nearly straight overhead.
  3. Lower back down with control until the handles/dumbbells are back at ear level.
Common mistakes
  • Arching your lower back to press the last few inches — keep your back flat against the pad throughout
12 Lateral Raise (Dumbbell) Isolation ›
Side shoulders

Raising light dumbbells out to your sides to build shoulder width. This is a control exercise, not a strength one — go lighter than you think and focus on smooth movement.

How to do it — step by step
  1. Stand holding a light dumbbell in each hand at your sides, slight bend in the elbows.
  2. Raise both arms out to the sides until roughly level with your shoulders — like making a "T" shape.
  3. Pause briefly, then lower slowly back down. Don't let momentum swing the weights up.
13 Triceps Pushdown (Cable) Isolation ›
Triceps

Standing at a cable machine with a bar or rope attachment set at the top, pushing the attachment down by straightening your arms.

How to do it — step by step
  1. Stand facing the cable machine, grip the bar/rope with elbows tucked close to your sides.
  2. Without moving your upper arms, push the attachment down until your arms are fully extended.
  3. Squeeze briefly, then let it rise back to the start under control.

Only your forearms should move — your elbows stay pinned at your sides throughout.

14 Lat Pulldown (Cable) Pull ›
Upper backBiceps

A seated cable machine where you pull a wide bar down to your chest, building the upper back muscles that counteract the forward hunch from sitting at a desk all day.

How to do it — step by step
  1. Sit with your thighs secured under the pads, grip the bar slightly wider than shoulder width.
  2. Lean back very slightly (not excessively), chest up.
  3. Pull the bar down to your upper chest by driving your elbows down and back — imagine trying to put your elbows in your back pockets.
  4. Pause briefly, then let the bar rise back up under control until your arms are extended.
Common mistakes
  • Leaning back excessively and using body weight to "cheat" the bar down — keep the lean minimal
  • Pulling the bar behind your neck — always pull to the front, to your upper chest
15 Seated Cable Row / Single-Arm Row Pull ›
Mid-backBiceps

Sitting at a cable row station (or standing, for a single-arm dumbbell row supported on a bench) and pulling a handle toward your torso. Builds the pulling strength that balances your pressing work.

How to do it — step by step (seated cable version)
  1. Sit with knees slightly bent, feet braced on the platform, grip the handle with arms extended.
  2. Sit up tall — this is your starting posture and it shouldn't change.
  3. Pull the handle toward your stomach, squeezing your shoulder blades together at the end.
  4. Let your arms extend back out under control, without letting your back round forward.
Single-arm dumbbell row version

Place one knee and one hand on a flat bench for support, holding a dumbbell in the other hand, arm hanging straight down. Pull the dumbbell up toward your hip, keeping your back flat (don't twist your torso), then lower under control.

Common mistakes
  • Rocking your torso back and forth to add momentum — keep your torso still, let your arms and back do the work
16 Bicep Curl (Dumbbell) Isolation ›
Biceps

The classic arm exercise — curling dumbbells up toward your shoulders.

How to do it — step by step
  1. Stand holding a dumbbell in each hand, arms hanging at your sides, palms facing forward.
  2. Without swinging your body, curl the dumbbells up toward your shoulders, keeping your elbows pinned at your sides.
  3. Squeeze briefly at the top, then lower slowly back to the start.
17 Front Plank (and Shoulder-Tap Variation) CoreBack-friendly ›
Whole coreShoulders (stabilizing)

Holding a straight-line position supported on your forearms and toes. This builds the core stability that directly protects your lower back — far more useful for back health than crunches, which actually load the spine in a way you want to avoid right now.

How to do it — step by step
  1. Lie face down, then prop yourself up on your forearms (elbows directly under shoulders) and toes.
  2. Lift your hips so your body forms one straight line from head to heels.
  3. Squeeze your glutes and brace your abs as if about to be punched in the stomach.
  4. Hold this position, breathing normally, for the prescribed time.
Shoulder-tap variation (Friday)

From the same plank position (hands on the floor instead of forearms, like the top of a push-up), alternately tap your opposite shoulder with each hand while keeping your hips as still as possible. This adds an anti-rotation challenge.

Common mistakes
  • Letting your hips sag toward the floor — keep the straight line
  • Piking your hips up too high — same fix, find the straight line
  • Holding your breath — breathe normally throughout
18 Side Plank CoreBack-friendly ›
Obliques (side core)

Builds lateral (side) core strength, which is commonly weak in people with low back pain and is rarely trained by standard ab exercises.

How to do it — step by step
  1. Lie on your side, propped up on one forearm, elbow directly under your shoulder.
  2. Stack your feet on top of each other (or stagger them for more stability).
  3. Lift your hips off the floor so your body forms a straight line from head to feet.
  4. Hold, then repeat on the other side.
19 Dead Bug CoreBack-care priority ›
Deep coreSpinal stability

One of the best-researched exercises specifically for low back pain prevention and rehab. It trains your core to keep your spine still while your arms and legs move — exactly the skill that protects your back during real-world activities.

How to do it — step by step
  1. Lie on your back, arms reaching straight up toward the ceiling, knees bent at 90° directly above your hips (like a tabletop position).
  2. Press your lower back flat into the floor/mat and keep it pressed down for the entire set — this is the most important part.
  3. Slowly lower one arm overhead and the opposite leg straight out toward the floor at the same time.
  4. Stop just before your lower back wants to arch up off the floor.
  5. Return to the start and repeat on the other side.

The entire point of this exercise is the flat back. If your lower back arches off the floor at any point, you've moved your arm/leg too far — bring the range in smaller until you can keep your back glued down throughout.

20 Bird Dog CoreBack-care priority ›
Deep coreBalanceGlutes

A clinically recommended exercise for chronic low back pain. Like Dead Bug, it trains spinal stability — but from a hands-and-knees position instead of lying down.

How to do it — step by step
  1. Start on hands and knees, hands under shoulders, knees under hips. Keep your back flat — neither sagging nor arched.
  2. Brace your core, then slowly extend one arm straight forward and the opposite leg straight back at the same time.
  3. Keep your hips and shoulders level — don't let either side rotate up or down.
  4. Hold for 2 seconds, then return to the start with control.
  5. Repeat on the other side.

Move slowly. The value here is in the control and the stillness of your trunk, not the speed.

21 Glute Bridge Warm-up / Back-care ›
GlutesHamstrings

A simpler, bodyweight version of the hip thrust — used here as a warm-up to activate your glutes before heavier lower-body lifting.

How to do it — step by step
  1. Lie on your back, knees bent, feet flat on the floor close to your glutes.
  2. Squeeze your glutes and lift your hips up until your body forms a straight line from shoulders to knees.
  3. Hold briefly at the top, then lower back down with control.
22 Interval Cardio (Treadmill or Cross-Trainer) Cardio ›
Cardiovascular fitness

Alternating short bursts of harder effort with short recovery periods. This builds fitness faster than a constant, steady pace — and Wednesday's session is built entirely around this format.

On a treadmill

For the "hard" interval: walk fast or jog lightly, optionally with the incline raised to 3–5% to increase effort without needing to run. For the "easy" interval: drop back to a comfortable, relaxed walking pace.

On a cross-trainer

For the "hard" interval: increase resistance and/or push the pace until breathing is noticeably harder. For the "easy" interval: drop resistance and slow down to a relaxed pace.

A simple way to judge effort: during the hard interval you should be breathing too hard to speak in full sentences. During the easy interval you should be able to hold a conversation comfortably.

23 General Warm-up Moves (Arm Circles, Leg Swings, Cat-Cow) Warm-up ›
Arm circles

Stand with arms extended out to your sides. Make small circles, gradually increasing the size, for the prescribed time, then reverse direction.

Leg swings

Hold onto something for balance. Swing one leg forward and backward in a controlled motion, like a pendulum, for the prescribed reps, then switch legs.

Cat-Cow (spinal mobility)

On hands and knees: inhale as you drop your belly down and lift your chest and tailbone up (Cow), then exhale as you round your spine up toward the ceiling, tucking your chin (Cat). Move slowly between the two positions, syncing with your breath.

Band/cable pull-aparts

Hold a light resistance band (or use a cable station with a rope handle) with arms extended in front of you at shoulder height. Pull your hands apart, squeezing your shoulder blades together, then return slowly.
