§1Mindset & Motivation

You are not starting over. You are resurfacing.

Getting laid off feels like a verdict. It isn't. It's a change in company circumstances. You have 12 years of compound learning — production incidents you debugged at 2am, architectural decisions you made and lived with, real code running in real systems. No interview can give you that. You already have the thing they're testing for.

Your only job for the next 45 days is to make what you know visible. Companies hire people whose existing knowledge fits a pattern they recognize. Your prep is about fitting that pattern — not about becoming a different engineer.

"The gap between a rejected candidate and a hired one is usually confidence and framing, not knowledge."

One day off at the start is fine. Then treat this like the most important project of 2026 — because it is.

The mental model that will carry you through

Consistency > Intensity
6 focused hours every day beats one 14-hour marathon. Schedule the same start time daily. Your brain learns in patterns. Don't miss two days in a row — missing one is human, missing two is a new habit.
Active Recall > Re-reading
Don't read your old notes. Explain the concept out loud, write the code from scratch, answer the question before Googling it. The friction is the learning. This is how your sk-int reference site should be used — test yourself first, read second.
ROI Thinking
System design is asked in every senior interview. HashMap internals is asked in 80% of Java interviews. Skip Lists are asked in 2%. Time is finite — allocate it where it returns offers. This plan does that math for you.
Parallel Job Search
Apply from Day 1, not Day 45. Early applications = early rejections = calibration data about what companies actually ask. Schedule 1hr/day for LinkedIn outreach, applications, and follow-ups. Never go a day without applying to at least 2 roles.
Daily anchor Every morning, before you open any study material, write down one sentence: what you'll accomplish today that moves you closer to an offer. Not "study Java" — "be able to explain HashMap resizing and ConcurrentHashMap segment locks from memory." Specificity makes progress visible.

§2The Strategy

You're targeting senior backend / Java / distributed systems roles. Here's what the typical interview loop at a good tech company actually tests, and how often:

Round What they test How often Your current level
Technical Phone Screen Java core, OOP, collections, 1-2 easy DSA problems Always Strong — 12 yrs daily use
DSA Coding Round 1 Arrays, strings, HashMap, Two-pointer, Sliding window Always Needs refreshing
DSA Coding Round 2 Trees, graphs, BFS/DFS, DP basics Very common Needs focused work
System Design Scale, databases, caching, messaging, microservices Always (Sr. roles) Strong — real experience
Java / Spring Deep Dive Concurrency, JVM, Spring IoC/AOP, JPA, transactions Very common Strong — needs articulation
Architecture / Design Microservices patterns, API design, CQRS, Saga Common (Sr. roles) Strong from experience
Behavioral / Leadership STAR stories, conflict, ownership, failure recovery Always Rich material from 12 yrs
The honest truth about DSA At 12 years of experience, companies understand your DSA won't be LeetCode-sharp. But you must be able to comfortably solve medium-difficulty problems in Java within 30–40 minutes. The bar is: clean code, correct approach, space/time analysis, and the ability to discuss trade-offs. You don't need hard DP. You do need medium trees, graphs, and hash maps.

What to skip entirely

From your sk-int site — skip or spend minimal time on: GraalVM, Quarkus, Micronaut (niche, rarely asked), L4/L5/L6 levels of any topic (too deep for interviews), Frontend Build Tools, TypeScript in depth (you're a backend candidate — JS/TS basics only), Advanced Algorithms (Network Flow, Approximation, Randomized), OS theory at L4+, Git internals. These have near-zero ROI for a senior backend Java interview in the next 45 days.

§7Weekly Review System

Every Saturday morning, before your mock session, spend 45 minutes answering these questions honestly:

  • What were the 3 most important things I learned this week? (write them down)
  • What's one concept I still can't explain clearly? (this goes at the top of next week's list)
  • How many DSA problems did I solve? How many felt genuinely comfortable? How many stumped me?
  • Did I apply to at least 10 jobs this week? If not, why not?
  • Did I do at least one mock interview or recorded self-explanation?
  • What's my energy level? Am I burning out, or do I have more to give?
  • What am I most nervous about in an actual interview right now? Study that next week.
The review is not optional Without a weekly review, you'll spend 6 weeks studying your strong areas while your actual gaps grow untouched. The review is what turns effort into calibrated progress. Treat it like a standup with yourself — honest, specific, and action-oriented.

§8Job Search — Parallel Track

Apply from Day 1. Interviews that happen in Week 2 are low-pressure calibration. Interviews in Week 5 are where you'll be sharp. You need both.

Where to apply
LinkedIn Jobs (set alerts), Naukri, Instahyre, AngelList for startups, company career pages for target companies directly. Also: your LinkedIn network — message 3 people per day (ex-colleagues, managers, college alumni) with a specific ask, not a generic "please help". "Hey [name], I'm exploring backend Java roles — do you know of any openings at [company]?" converts better than "I'm open to work."
Your edge over other candidates
12 years means you've seen production incidents, scaled systems, made architecture decisions, led and mentored others. Explicitly surface this in every conversation. "In my last role, we had to migrate a monolith to microservices under live traffic — I drove that decision" is worth 10 LeetCode problems in a senior interview. Prepare 10 specific stories with outcomes.

STAR Stories to prepare (your 12-year advantage)

  • A time you diagnosed and fixed a major production incident. What was the root cause? What did you do differently after?
  • A technical decision you made that you'd now make differently. What did you learn?
  • A system design you drove end-to-end — what constraints shaped the architecture?
  • A time you had to push back on a technical decision from above. How did you handle it?
  • A time you improved team velocity or code quality significantly. What was the before/after?
  • A time you mentored a junior developer. What was your approach and what was the outcome?
  • Your biggest professional failure. What did it cost, and what did it build?
  • A time you worked under significant deadline or technical debt pressure. How did you prioritize?
The offer comes from interviews, not studying Studying alone will not get you a job. Applying every day, doing real mock interviews every week, and getting into actual interview loops will. The plan above is aggressive but realistic. If you follow it even 80%, you will be sharply prepared by mid-August. Go get it.

The next 45 days will feel uneven. Some days you'll crush 3 LeetCode problems and feel unstoppable. Other days you'll read the same paragraph four times and remember nothing. Both are normal. The only dangerous day is the one where you decide "I'll restart tomorrow" — that's how weeks disappear.
