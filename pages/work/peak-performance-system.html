§1The 20% That Drives 80% of Results

Most performance advice is noise. These five things, done consistently, compound into a measurably different person in 45 days. Everything else in this guide is supporting detail.

1
Sleep 7.5–8 hours. Non-negotiable.
The highest-leverage intervention available to a human being. Nothing else comes close.
Highest ROI
Sleep is when your brain consolidates everything you studied that day into long-term memory. The hippocampus replays the day's learning during slow-wave sleep and transfers it to the neocortex. Cutting sleep from 8 to 6 hours reduces cognitive performance by 30% — equivalent to being legally drunk. You cannot study your way out of a sleep deficit. A 6-hour study day with 8 hours sleep retains more than a 10-hour study day with 5 hours sleep. Go to bed at the same time every night, even weekends. Set a hard cutoff: no screens 45 minutes before bed. This one habit carries more interview performance weight than 2 extra hours of studying.
📚 Matthew Walker "Why We Sleep" (2017) · Stickgold & Walker (2005) Nature Reviews Neuroscience · Harvard Medical School Sleep & Learning studies
2
30 minutes of movement every morning.
Increases BDNF — brain-derived neurotrophic factor — the fertiliser for new neural connections.
Brain Booster
Exercise before studying increases blood flow to the prefrontal cortex (decision-making, focus) and hippocampus (memory). BDNF released during aerobic exercise literally helps neurons grow and connect — John Ratey called it "Miracle-Gro for the brain." A 20–30 minute brisk walk before your first study block improves focus and memory retention for the next 2–4 hours. You don't need a gym. Walk briskly for 30 minutes after waking. Leave your phone at home. Let your mind wander — this is when your subconscious processes yesterday's learning and when insight on stuck problems most often appears.
📚 John Ratey "Spark" (2008) · Hillman et al. (2008) Nature Reviews Neuroscience · Van Praag et al. (1999) Nature Neuroscience
3
Eat breakfast with protein within 1 hour of waking.
Sets your neurotransmitter baseline — dopamine, serotonin, acetylcholine — for the whole day.
Fuel
Skipping breakfast or eating only simple carbs (bread, biscuits) causes a blood sugar spike followed by a crash in 90–120 minutes — right when you should be in your first deep study block. Protein + complex carbs + fat at breakfast creates sustained glucose release for 3–4 hours of sharp focus. Target: 25–30g of protein at breakfast. Eggs (3 whole eggs = ~18g), dal + rice, paneer, Greek yoghurt + nuts. Add a complex carb: oats, brown rice, chapati. Add fat: nuts, ghee. This is not a diet — it is neurochemical infrastructure for everything else in your day.
📚 Gómez-Pinilla (2008) Nature Reviews Neuroscience · Benton & Parker (1998) Appetite · multiple breakfast-cognition RCTs
4
Protect 90-minute deep work blocks. No phone. No tabs.
The brain reaches peak focus at ~23 minutes and sustains it for 90 minutes before needing recovery.
Focus
When you shift attention (check your phone, switch tabs), your brain releases a small dopamine hit that makes the distraction habit stronger. Worse: it takes an average of 23 minutes to fully re-enter a state of deep focus after an interruption (Gloria Mark, UC Irvine). In a 2-hour study block, one phone check effectively costs you half the session. Phone in another room. One browser tab. 90-minute blocks. This alone can double the effective output of your study hours without adding a single minute.
📚 Gloria Mark "Attention Span" (2023) · Cal Newport "Deep Work" (2016) · Ulrich & Keil (2014) attention restoration theory
5
Review what you learned within 24 hours.
The Ebbinghaus forgetting curve: without review, you forget 70% of new information within 24 hours.
Retention
A single 10-minute review within 24 hours reduces decay from 70% to under 20%. Spending 30 minutes reviewing yesterday's material before starting today's new content is more effective than 30 extra minutes of new study. The optimal review schedule: Day 1 → Day 3 → Day 7 → Day 14 → Day 30. Use Anki cards or write 5 key points from yesterday in a notebook each morning. This is the mechanism behind spaced repetition — the most research-validated technique in all of learning science, bar none.
📚 Ebbinghaus (1885) · Cepeda et al. (2006) Psychological Bulletin meta-analysis · Kornell (2009) Psychological Science

§2Sleep — The Master Variable

Sleep is the variable that affects every other variable: memory, mood, focus, immune function, stress response, creativity, decision-making. It is also the most commonly sacrificed in crunch periods — which is exactly backwards.

Sleep hygiene essentials
Same bed/wake time daily — even weekends. Your circadian rhythm doesn't recognise weekdays; irregularity is the single biggest disruptor. Dark room — even a crack of light reduces melatonin. Cool room (18–20°C) — your core temperature must drop 1–2°C to initiate sleep. No screens 45–60 min before bed — blue light suppresses melatonin by 50% for 3 hours after exposure.
Pre-sleep wind-down (30 min)
9:30 PM: stop all study content. 9:45 PM: dim all lights (physically signals brain to produce melatonin). 10:00 PM: write tomorrow's 3 priorities — this offloads the mental rehearsal loop that keeps you awake at night. 10:15 PM: read fiction or light non-fiction. 10:30 PM: lights out. This sequence takes 60 days to feel automatic but works from day one.
The nap advantage
A 20-minute nap (set alarm for 25 min) between 1–3 PM restores alertness to morning levels without disrupting night sleep. NASA research: 26-minute nap → 26% improvement in performance. If you feel the post-lunch crash, nap instead of pushing through with caffeine. The crash is your circadian dip — real, physiological, not fixable by willpower. A short nap is the correct response.
What destroys sleep quality
Alcohol — even 1 drink cuts REM sleep by 25%, destroying memory consolidation. Caffeine after 2 PM — half-life of 5–7 hrs means 2 PM coffee = half still active at 9 PM. Irregular wake time — the biggest circadian disruptor. Intense exercise within 3 hours of bed — raises core temperature, delays sleep onset 30–90 min. Heavy dinner late — raises blood glucose, increases core temperature.
The sleep-learning link During slow-wave sleep, your hippocampus replays the day's experiences at 20x speed and transfers them to the neocortex for long-term storage. During REM, your brain makes novel connections between concepts — this is where insight and understanding happen. The advice "sleep on a problem" is neuroscientifically accurate. Study something, then sleep, then review: this three-step sequence is the most effective learning protocol known.

§3Movement & Exercise

You already have a workout plan. Here is the cognitive performance angle — why movement is not just fitness but directly upgrades your study quality:

🚶
Morning walk — 30 min, no phone
Before your first study block. Natural light in the first 30 min anchors your circadian rhythm and triggers cortisol to peak at the right time. The default mode network (creative, associative thinking) activates during rhythmic walking — problems you're stuck on often resolve themselves here without conscious effort.
⏰
Movement breaks — every 90 min
Stand up. Walk 5 minutes. Do 10 squats or push-ups. Blood flow to the brain drops measurably after 90 minutes of sitting. These micro-breaks reset focus and actually increase total cognitive output across the day — more effective total work hours than grinding through fatigue.
💪
Gym plan — keep it running
Resistance training reduces cortisol, improves sleep quality, increases testosterone and growth hormone (both support memory and energy), and provides the psychological identity of "I'm someone who takes care of myself." Don't skip gym sessions during high-stress interview periods — that is precisely when you need them most.
The walking insight phenomenon Stanford research (Oppezzo & Schwartz, 2014) found creative output increased by 81% while walking vs sitting. Your brain in "rest" during a walk is in its most associative, connective state. Walk with a system design problem. Sit down to write the solution. The two modes of thinking need different environments.

§5What Hurts Cognitive Performance

Food / Habit Why it hurts performance
White sugar + refined carbs Blood glucose spike → crash in 60–90 min. Crash = brain fog, irritability, inability to focus. The "2 PM slump" is mostly sugar-driven.
Heavy oily lunch High-fat meals divert blood to digestive system. Cognitive performance measurably drops for 2–3 hours after. Save heavy meals for dinner — not lunch.
Skipping meals Blood glucose drops below 70 mg/dL → brain fog, inability to concentrate, anxiety. Your brain has no glucose storage — it needs a consistent supply every 3–4 hours.
Alcohol (even 1 drink) Disrupts REM sleep even at low doses. GABA activation impairs hippocampal memory formation for 24+ hours. Avoid entirely during the 45-day preparation period.
Ultra-processed snacks (chips, biscuits) Zero nutritional value to the brain. Spike blood sugar, increase systemic inflammation markers. Replace with nuts, fruit, or curd.
Caffeine after 2 PM Caffeine half-life is 5–7 hours. 2 PM coffee = half still blocking adenosine receptors at 9 PM = fragmented, lower-quality sleep = next day cognitive performance drops.
Late heavy dinner (after 8 PM) Raises blood glucose and core temperature at precisely the wrong time for sleep onset. Light dinner before 8 PM is one of the easiest sleep quality improvements available.
The meal timing principle Eat your biggest meal at lunch, not dinner. Your digestive system follows its own circadian rhythm — most efficient at midday, least efficient late evening. A heavy dinner at 9 PM raises blood glucose, increases body temperature, and fragments sleep. You will feel the difference within 3 days of switching.

§6Hydration & Caffeine Strategy

💧 Water — the most underrated cognitive enhancer
A 1% drop in hydration causes measurable cognitive decline. A 2% drop causes a 13% reduction in short-term memory. Most people are chronically mildly dehydrated and attribute the brain fog to something else. Target: 2.5–3L water per day. Practical system: fill a 1L bottle on your desk at 7am, 11am, and 3pm. Finish each before the next. Dark yellow urine = dehydrated. Pale yellow = correctly hydrated.
☕ Caffeine — how to use it right
The 90-minute delay rule: don't drink caffeine until 90 minutes after waking. Your cortisol peaks naturally in the first 90 minutes — caffeine here builds tolerance without benefit. Use at 9–10 AM for your deep study block. Cut off at 1–2 PM. Maximum 1–2 cups per day. The L-theanine + caffeine combination (green tea, or coffee + green tea together) gives calm, focused alertness without the jitters — the most well-studied cognitive performance stack available.

§7Memory & Retention Science

The techniques with the strongest research backing for long-term retention. Not reading strategies. Not highlighting. The actual cognitive tools that work:

🎯
Active Recall — test yourself, never re-read
Close the material. Write or say everything you remember. Check. This creates a retrieval path — each retrieval strengthens the memory trace. Re-reading creates the illusion of knowing without the path. After reading any section: close it, explain the concept out loud, then check. This single technique makes retention 2–3x better than passive review.
Memory +200%Roediger & Karpicke 2006
📅
Spaced Repetition — review at expanding intervals
Review on Day 1 → Day 3 → Day 7 → Day 14 → Day 30. Each review before forgetting resets the decay curve at a higher baseline. Use Anki (free) — it automatically schedules reviews at the optimal interval. Create one card per key concept. After 2 weeks of this, you will notice you can recall Java concurrency details effortlessly. This is not magic — it is the mechanism.
Long-term retentionEbbinghaus 1885, Cepeda 2006
🗣️
Feynman Technique — explain it simply
Pick a concept (e.g. "how ConcurrentHashMap works"). Explain it out loud as if teaching a 12-year-old. Where you stumble — those are your gaps. Go back and fill them. Repeat until explanation is smooth. This directly prepares you for the interview context where you must explain complex things clearly. Teaching is the best form of learning because simplification forces deep understanding.
Deep understandingInterview prep
🔗
Interleaving — mix subjects, do not block-study
Studying Java for 3 hrs, then system design for 3 hrs feels efficient but leads to "blocked" learning that doesn't transfer. Interleaving — Java for 90 min, then system design 90 min, then DSA 90 min — feels harder but produces significantly better retention and pattern recognition. The difficulty is the learning. Your 45-day plan already does this — lean into it.
Transfer learningKornell & Bjork 2008
✍️
Write and draw by hand — not just type
When learning a complex concept (system design, concurrency model), draw it by hand on paper. Handwriting activates more brain regions than typing and forces summarisation (you process to select what to write). Drawing a system architecture also directly prepares you for whiteboard interviews. Buy a notebook. Use it daily for "explain by drawing." This is the single most underrated study technique among engineers.

§8Deep Focus & Concentration

The 90-minute ultradian rhythm
Your brain naturally cycles through 90-minute focus–recovery periods (Peretz Lavie, chronobiology). Working with these cycles — 90 minutes fully on, 15–20 minutes fully off — produces more total output than grinding for 4 continuous hours of diminishing focus. When the timer rings, close the laptop and move. Don't "just finish this thing" — that is how the cycles break and fatigue accumulates.
Phone = attention capital destroyed
Having a smartphone visible on the desk (even face-down, even off) reduces available cognitive capacity by 10–20% — your brain uses resources to resist checking it. The solution: phone in another room during all study blocks. This is not discipline — this is removing the trigger. Use a cheap secondary device as a study timer if needed. The best focus hack is environmental, not motivational.
Background audio
For deep work (new concepts): silence or brown noise. For mechanical tasks (reviewing notes, writing code you already know): lo-fi instrumental is fine. Lyrics in any language activate your language processing centres — competing directly with reading and thinking. The rule: if your task requires language, your audio should not have language.
The 5-4-3-2-1 start trigger
When you sit down and feel resistant — count backwards from 5, then physically begin. Open the first page, read the first line, write the first word. Resistance is always before starting, not during. The brain's desire to avoid discomfort is strongest in the moment before action. Five seconds is the window. Count. Start. Everything else follows.

§9Daily Non-Negotiables

Six habits done every day. Small individually. Compounding collectively. 45 days of all six is worth more than one week of perfect performance followed by collapse.

☀️
Morning sunlight within 30 minutes of waking
10–15 minutes of natural outdoor light anchors your circadian rhythm, triggers cortisol to peak at the right time, and sets your melatonin release 14–16 hours later — meaning you feel sleepy at the correct time at night. This single habit fixes most sleep timing issues. It is free, takes 10 minutes, and requires no phone. Step outside or stand by an open window while drinking water.
Circadian anchorCortisol calibrationMood baseline
📓
Morning journal — 5 minutes only
Three things exactly: (1) One specific thing you are grateful for. (2) Today's single most important outcome. (3) One thing you are anxious about — name it. Anxiety named loses half its power by activating the labelling response in the prefrontal cortex (Lieberman, UCLA). This clears the mental RAM that would otherwise run in the background competing with your focus all morning.

🧘
10 minutes of breath-focused meditation
Not spiritual practice — nervous system regulation. Box breathing (4 counts in, hold 4, out 6) activates the parasympathetic nervous system, reduces cortisol, and directly improves prefrontal cortex function. After 10 days of daily practice, fMRI studies show measurable increases in grey matter density in the PFC. You don't need an app. Sit upright, count your breaths to 10, restart when you lose count. The practice is the returning, not the staying.

📋
End-of-day review + tomorrow's 3 tasks
Before closing the laptop: write what you accomplished, one thing that surprised you, and 3 specific tasks for tomorrow in order. This prevents the Zeigarnik effect — unfinished tasks loop through your mind during sleep, fragmenting rest. Writing them down signals to your brain they are handled and can be released. Also means you wake up with zero decision fatigue — you know exactly what to do first.

Phone-free first 60 minutes of every day
Checking notifications first thing puts you in a reactive state — responding to other people's agendas — before orienting to your own. Your first waking moments set the attention tone for the entire day. Start proactively (your plan, your priorities, your morning ritual) rather than reactively. This single habit is cited by virtually every high-performance person in research studies as the most impactful change they made. It costs nothing and starts tomorrow.

Cold end to morning shower (30–60 seconds)
Cold water exposure triggers a 2.5x sustained increase in dopamine (not a spike — a sustained elevation lasting 2–4 hours), norepinephrine (focus), and epinephrine (alertness). The deeper benefit: tolerating a controllable uncomfortable experience and calming yourself through it trains your nervous system's resilience response. Interviews are uncomfortable. Practicing discomfort tolerance daily makes you better at performing under it. Turn cold for 30–60 seconds at the end of every shower.

§10Morning & Evening Ritual

Rituals remove decisions. When the morning sequence is automatic, you don't waste willpower deciding what to do next — you execute. Build this until it feels as natural as brushing your teeth.

Morning

5:45
Wake. No phone. 500ml water immediately.
You have been fasting 7–8 hours. Your brain is dehydrated. Rehydrate before caffeine. Step outside for 10–15 min of natural light while drinking water.
6:00
5-min journal + yesterday's key learning (spaced review)
3 gratitudes, today's most important outcome, one anxiety named. Then read your 5 key points from yesterday — this is your daily spaced repetition review built into the ritual.
6:05
10-min meditation or box breathing
4 counts in, hold 4, 6 counts out. Or simply count breaths to 10 and restart. No app required. Sit upright. The practice is returning attention when it wanders — the same skill as sustained study focus.
6:15
30-min brisk walk (no phone)
BDNF dose, cortisol calibration, creative thinking time. Hold the problem you will study today lightly in mind — solutions often surface during rhythmic movement without deliberate effort.
6:45

90 minutes after waking = optimal caffeine window. Your cortisol has already peaked naturally. Now caffeine adds to it rather than replacing it (which builds tolerance faster). Eat: eggs, oats, paneer, nuts.
7:00
Study begins. Phone in another room.
You have done everything that optimises your brain for learning before opening a single resource. Your first 90-minute block is your highest-quality learning window of the day. Use it for the hardest new material.
Evening
The Wind-Down Protocol — protect sleep quality
6:00 PM
End all new learning. Switch to light review only.
Your brain's ability to encode new long-term memories decreases in the evening. This is the time for writing flashcards, reviewing what you've already learned, or reading engineering blogs. Not new topics.
7:00 PM
Light dinner — dal-rice, khichdi, idli-sambar.
Finish eating before 8 PM. Light dinner = lower blood glucose at bedtime = better sleep quality = better memory consolidation = sharper tomorrow.

No screens. Dim lights. Physical book or conversation.

Consistency of sleep timing matters more than total hours in the short term. The same time even on weekends is more restorative than trying to "catch up" by sleeping 10 hours on Saturday (which partially works but disrupts Monday's rhythm).

§11Building Discipline — What It Actually Is

Discipline is not a personality trait. It is not willpower. It is not motivation. It is a skill — specifically, the skill of starting despite not feeling like it. Here is what research says actually builds it:

The identity shift
"I'm trying to study" is weaker than "I'm a person who studies every morning." Behaviour follows identity. After each session, acknowledge it: "I showed up today." After 7 days: "I'm someone who shows up." This is how the brain builds self-concept, which then governs behaviour automatically — without willpower. (James Clear, Atomic Habits — this is the core mechanism.)
Reduce activation energy to near zero
The biggest barrier to a habit is starting it. Remove every friction: laptop open and charged the night before. Notebook and pen on the desk. First resource already open in the browser. First task already written on a sticky note. When starting requires zero decisions, you start. When it requires five small decisions, the resistance wins.
Never miss twice
Missing one day doesn't break a habit. Missing two days in a row does — the second miss creates a new pattern. You will miss a day during 45 days. That is expected. The rule: never let it become two consecutive days. A 30-minute session on a bad day counts. Progress over perfection is not a platitude — it is the functional definition of habit formation.
Temptation bundling
Only listen to your favourite podcast or music during your morning walk. Only have your favourite tea while doing your evening review. Only watch a show after completing your daily study target. The brain learns to associate the reward with the behaviour. Over time, the behaviour itself becomes rewarding. This is the mechanism that makes habits feel effortless — they pair with something already desired.
On motivation — the honest truth Motivation follows action; it does not precede it. Waiting to feel motivated before studying is waiting for a bus that runs on a different schedule. Do the action first. The motivation and positive emotion come after the first 5–10 minutes of genuine engagement. This is not a metaphor — it is the neuroscience of how dopamine is released during goal-directed activity. Start. Everything else follows from starting.

§12Stress & Nervous System Management

Being laid off is a real stressor — your nervous system does not distinguish between job loss and physical threat. Chronically elevated cortisol impairs the hippocampus (memory), prefrontal cortex (decisions), and immune system. Managing the stress response is not self-care. It is performance infrastructure.

🫁
Physiological sigh — fastest nervous system reset available
Double inhale through the nose (in, then in again), followed by a long slow exhale through the mouth. This deflates collapsed alveoli (air sacs in the lungs), rapidly reduces CO2, and calms the amygdala within 1–2 repetitions. Takes 5 seconds. Use before an interview, when stuck on a problem, when anxiety spikes, or when you feel overwhelmed. Discovered and validated by Andrew Huberman's lab at Stanford.
Immediate calmVagus nerveHuberman Lab, Stanford
🎯
Name what you feel — the labelling effect
Neuroscience (Lieberman, UCLA) shows that labelling an emotion "I feel anxious about this interview" reduces amygdala activation measurably within seconds. Putting feelings into words down-regulates the emotional brain and activates the reasoning brain. When anxiety hits: say or write "I feel anxious because [specific thing]." Then: "What would I tell a friend feeling this way?" This is cognitive reappraisal — the most evidence-backed emotional regulation strategy available.
Amygdala regulationPFC activationLieberman et al., UCLA
Reframe: stress as performance fuel Kelly McGonigal (Stanford, "The Upside of Stress") documented: people told "stress is performance-enhancing" before a stressful task performed better and had healthier cardiovascular responses than those told "stress is harmful." The stress response itself didn't change — the interpretation did. When you feel pre-interview anxiety: "My body is preparing me. This arousal is energy I can direct." This reframe, done sincerely and repeatedly, measurably improves performance. It is accurate biology — the sympathetic nervous system activation that causes anxiety is the same one that creates peak athletic performance.

§13Confidence & Self-Belief

Evidence-based confidence — not affirmations
Confidence that sticks comes from accumulated evidence of your own competence. Every evening, write one specific thing you did well today — solved a LeetCode problem, explained a concept clearly, completed your plan. After 14 days, you have 14 concrete data points. When doubt hits ("I can't do this"), read the list back. You did. 14 times. This is how real confidence is built — not by telling yourself you're great, but by generating evidence and reading it back to yourself.
Your experience is your unfair advantage
You have 12 years of real production experience. Fresh graduates study the same topics from the same books and have zero real data. Every question is an opportunity to anchor your answer in reality: "In my last role, we implemented this because..." — that sentence is worth 10 textbook answers. Interviewers remember the candidate who connected theory to production reality. You have more production reality to connect than 95% of candidates in any given pool.
Posture is physiological feedback
Upright posture → higher testosterone, lower cortisol (physiological, not metaphorical). Slouching compresses the diaphragm → shallow breathing → anxiety feedback loop. Practice sitting and standing upright. Before an interview, stand tall with shoulders back for 2 minutes in a private space. The nervous system reads proprioceptive signals from your body continuously — give it signals that say "I am capable and prepared."
Rejection as calibration data
Every interview rejection is a signal, not a verdict. After each one, ask the recruiter for feedback (many will share). Map it to a specific gap. Fill the gap. Interview again. The first 3–5 interviews after years of not interviewing will not represent your ceiling. Treat them as training data, not make-or-break tests. Your performance will improve measurably with each iteration as you re-learn the format and pacing.

§14Environment Design

"You don't rise to the level of your goals, you fall to the level of your systems." Your environment shapes behaviour without willpower — design it deliberately:

  • Dedicated study space — same chair, same desk, every day. Your brain learns to associate the environment with focus. Don't study in bed (your brain associates the bed with sleep). One space, one purpose.
  • Desk clutter = cognitive load. Only what you need: one notebook, one pen, one water bottle, one resource. Visual clutter competes for the attention you need for learning. Clear the desk the night before.
  • Prepare the environment the night before. Laptop charged and open. Notebook on a fresh page. First task written on a sticky note. When you sit down in the morning, you start — you don't set up. The "set up first" phase is where many mornings silently collapse.
  • Communicate your study hours to your household — once, clearly. "Between 7 AM and 1 PM I'm unavailable unless it's urgent." Interruptions are devastating (23-minute recovery window). Your family respects what you communicate clearly; they cannot read what you don't say.
  • Capture notebook on the desk. When intrusive thoughts appear during study ("I need to call that person"), write them in the notebook and return to work immediately. The thought is captured and won't loop. Check the notebook only during breaks.
  • Decent noise-isolating earphones. Even a basic pair playing brown noise blocks construction sounds, TV in the next room, household conversations. Worth it immediately, available for under ₹500.

§15What to Eliminate — The Subtractives

These have high cognitive cost and low return. Removing even two or three frees up hours of mental bandwidth daily:

📱 Mindless social media scrolling
Instagram Reels, YouTube Shorts, Twitter feeds — engineered to maximise context switches per minute, rewiring your prefrontal cortex to demand novelty constantly. After 60 minutes of scrolling, sitting with one topic for 90 minutes feels physically difficult — because it is, neurologically. Set 20-minute social media windows (after lunch, after dinner) and use app timers to enforce them. Not forever — for 45 days.
📰 Excessive news
News is optimised for anxiety, not information. 90% of what you absorb in 30 minutes of news will be irrelevant to you in a week and will negatively impact your mood for hours. Check news once per day, maximum 15 minutes, after your study session. The important things will reach you through other people. The fear of "missing something" is the news industry's core product — and it has real cognitive costs when you need your brain to perform.
🔔 Decision fatigue from trivial choices
Every decision depletes the same mental resource you use for complex reasoning. Automate trivial decisions: rotating breakfast options, same gym days, same study start time, same morning sequence. Design trivial decisions away. This is not laziness — it is conserving executive function for the decisions that actually matter: the system design question, the algorithm problem, the behavioural story.
💬 Energy-draining social environments
