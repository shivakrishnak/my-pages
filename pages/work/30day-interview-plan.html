§1Mindset & ROI Strategy

Thirty days is enough — not because the material is small, but because you're not learning from zero. You're doing three things: resurfacing what 12 years already taught you, closing the one real gap (DSA fluency under time pressure), and rehearsing articulation so it comes out clearly in a room.

The ROI filter for everything below Your sk-int reference covers 40+ domains from L0 (orientation) to L6 (theory) — that's comprehensive by design, built for zero-to-mastery on each topic. For a 30-day sprint at your experience level, the job is ruthless filtering: pick the domains that get asked constantly at senior backend interviews, go to L2–L3 depth (not L5–L6), and skip domains that are low-probability for your specific target roles entirely.
Consistency > Intensity
6–7 focused hours daily, same structure, every day beats sporadic 12-hour cram days. Your brain compounds on repetition of structure, not heroic one-offs.
Depth over breadth, always
Explaining HashMap resizing and ConcurrentHashMap locking clearly beats a shallow tour of 10 topics. Depth is what interviewers actually probe for.
DSA is a skill, not a knowledge gap
You know the concepts. What needs rebuilding is speed and pattern recognition under a clock. That only comes from timed reps — not more reading.
Talk before you type
Every technical answer should be rehearsed out loud first. Interviews are a spoken-word test wearing a technical costume. Practice the spoken part deliberately.

The honest bar at 12 years experience Interviewers don't expect LeetCode-hard fluency from senior candidates — they expect clean code, the correct approach articulated first, and real trade-off discussion on medium-difficulty problems within 30–40 minutes. Skip Hard-tagged problems almost entirely this month; they have poor ROI relative to nailing the Critical/High rows above cold.

Daily DSA practice structure

1️⃣
Solve 2–3 problems/day from the pattern that week is focused on (see weekly breakdown below)
2️⃣
Speak the approach out loud before writing code — this is the actual interview skill, not silent coding
3️⃣
Time yourself — 25–30 min per medium problem, hard stop, then review the optimal solution regardless of outcome
4️⃣
Re-solve without looking 3 days later (spaced repetition) — pick 1 problem from 3 days ago each morning as warm-up

§5Sequencing Strategy — Why This Order

A pure linear plan — Week 1 = Java only, Week 2 = Concurrency only, Week 3 = System Design only — optimizes for learning progression, not for being interview-ready at an unknown cutoff date. If an interview lands after 2 weeks under that structure, you'd have deep Java knowledge and zero System Design — a real gap for senior roles, where System Design is often the highest-stakes round.

But touching a brand new topic every single day is its own problem — one day of exposure rarely produces real consolidation, especially for dense material like Concurrency internals or System Design deep-dives. So this plan uses 2-day topic blocks: enough focus time to actually retain the material, while still guaranteeing full breadth by day 7 and full depth by day 14.

The actual design rule Every week is built from three 2-day blocks, covering all 6 high-ROI categories — Java Core, Concurrency, Spring, System Design, Microservices + SQL, and DSA — grouped in pairs that naturally reinforce each other (e.g. Concurrency pairs with JVM; System Design pairs with Microservices patterns). Week 1's three blocks give real, consolidated coverage of everything. Week 2's three blocks deepen the same six categories using the same pairing logic. By day 14 you have genuine interview-readiness across the full breadth — not shallow exposure, but actual working knowledge in two-day doses. Weeks 3–4 convert that into true fluency and speed.

Within each 2-day block, day one is concept-building and day two is application + the DSA pattern for that block — so every block ends with something you can actually demonstrate, not just something you read. If a week gets interrupted, you've already banked a complete, usable block rather than a half-finished topic.

What "complete by day 30" means here By the end of week 4, every P1 topic from §2 has been covered at L2–L3 depth, and every Critical/High-ROI DSA pattern from §3 has been drilled multiple times with spaced repetition. Nothing in the P1 list is left untouched — the 2-day pairing only changes how depth accumulates on each topic, not whether it gets covered by day 30.

§7Weekly Review & Revise System

Every Sunday (light day) and the Tuesday closing each week, run this review honestly. This is what keeps the plan a living document instead of a wishlist you fall behind on by week 2.

  • What were the 3 most important things I learned this week? Write them down specifically.
  • What's one concept I still can't explain clearly out loud? That goes first in next week's queue.
  • How many DSA problems did I actually solve, and how many felt genuinely comfortable vs. still shaky?
  • Did I apply to at least 10 roles this week? If not, why — and what's the fix?
  • Did I do at least one mock interview or recorded self-explanation this week?
  • What's my energy level — am I burning out, or is there more to give next week?
  • Based on all of the above: what changes in next week's daily targets? (Revise the plan, don't just follow it blindly.)
Revising is not failing the plan If week 1 shows Java Concurrency needs more time than scheduled, pull a day from week 4's DSA volume and add it to week 2. This document is a living plan, not a locked contract — the discipline is in reviewing honestly every week, not in following the original schedule rigidly. A plan that adapts to real signal beats a perfect plan that ignores it.

You're not learning a new field. You're presenting one you already know.

Thirty days feels tight only if you imagine starting from zero. You're not. Every day this month is about making 12 years of real production experience visible and articulate — plus closing the one honest gap, which is coding-interview speed under a clock.

"The gap between a rejected senior candidate and a hired one is almost always articulation and pattern-recognition speed — not underlying knowledge."

Review honestly every week. Revise the plan when the signal says to. Show up daily, even on the hard days — a Minimum Viable Day is still a day you didn't lose.
