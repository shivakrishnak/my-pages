Your Weekly Plan

Built for general fitness, endurance, and strength — not bodybuilding. Five sessions a week, 45–60 minutes each, using the standard equipment available at Cult Neo Gym Mallapur (barbells, dumbbells, cable/functional trainer, smith machine or squat rack, leg press, lat pulldown, chest press, treadmill/cross-trainer).

This is an Upper/Lower split: two lower-body days, two upper-body days, and one dedicated cardio/conditioning day, with two rest days. Every session opens with a hip-hinge or core-stability move chosen specifically because of your lower back sensitivity — the goal is to build a strong, pain-resistant back, not just avoid aggravating it.

Why This Split

Five compound-lift-based sessions a week, alternating upper and lower body, give each muscle group roughly 48 hours to recover between sessions while still training everything twice a week — the rep range proven most reliable for building strength and muscle endurance together, per ACSM/NSCA general fitness guidelines.

Why So Few Exercises

5–6 exercises per day, all compound (multi-joint) movements. Research consistently shows a handful of well-executed compound lifts builds more real-world strength and fitness than 15 isolation exercises — and it's far easier to recover from and stay consistent with.

1. Goblet Squat (Dumbbell)

Compound
Sets3
Reps10–12
Weight12–16 kg DB
Rest75 sec
Hold one dumbbell vertically against your chest. Sit back and down, knees tracking over toes, chest tall, until thighs are roughly parallel to the floor. Keep your lower back neutral — don't round it at the bottom. ⚑ Back-friendly squat pattern — easier on the spine than a barbell back squat

2. Romanian Deadlift (Dumbbell)

Compound
Sets3
Reps10
Weight10–14 kg per DB
Rest90 sec
Hold dumbbells in front of thighs. Push hips back (not down) while keeping knees soft and back flat, lowering weights along your shins until you feel a hamstring stretch, then drive hips forward to stand. This is the single best exercise for building a resilient lower back — it strengthens the exact hip-hinge pattern you use when lifting your kids. Form priority: Start lighter than you think you need. Stop the descent the moment your back starts to round.

3. Leg Press (Machine)

Compound
Sets3
Reps12–15
WeightStart ~40kg, build
Rest75 sec
Feet shoulder-width on the platform. Lower until knees reach about 90°, press back up without locking knees out hard. Machine-supported, so it's very low-risk for your back while still building real leg strength.

4. Standing Calf Raise

Isolation
Sets3
Reps15
WeightBodyweight or DB in hand
Rest45 sec
On flat ground or a small step, rise onto your toes, pause 1 second, lower slowly. Builds ankle/calf endurance for walking, running, and daily stamina.

Saturday & Sunday — Rest / Active Recovery

Rest days are when your muscles actually repair and grow stronger — they're not optional, they're part of the plan.

What To Do

  • A relaxed 30–40 minute walk on one or both days
  • Light stretching, especially hips, hamstrings, and lower back
  • Playing with your kids counts as activity — no need to "do more"
  • Prioritize sleep — this is where most muscle recovery happens

What To Avoid

  • Heavy lifting or high-intensity training
  • Sitting completely still all day if avoidable — gentle movement helps back pain more than total rest
  • Skipping both rest days even if you feel good — recovery is when adaptation happens

Principles & Progression Rules

The rules that make this plan work over months, not just one week.

Progressive Overload — How To Add Weight
The 2-for-2 rule: If you complete all prescribed sets and reps with good form for 2 consecutive sessions on an exercise, add a small amount of weight next time — typically 2.5kg for upper body lifts and 2.5–5kg for lower body lifts. If form breaks down or you miss reps, stay at the same weight until you complete it cleanly.

Starting Weights

The weight numbers in this plan are sensible starting points for someone with some training experience, not fixed targets. Your first 1–2 weeks should feel like a "could do a few more reps" effort — this lets you learn each movement pattern safely before adding real load. Adjust up or down in week 1 based on how each lift actually feels.

Tracking Progress

Keep a simple note on your phone: date, exercise, weight, reps completed. Five minutes a week of tracking is what turns "going to the gym" into measurable, lasting strength and fitness gains.

Rest Between Sets

The rest periods listed (45–90 seconds) are based on the goal of each exercise: compound lower-body lifts need more recovery (75–90s) than isolation or core work (45s). Resting properly is part of the program, not wasted time.

Nutrition & Sleep Basics

No exercise program outperforms poor sleep or poor eating. Aim for 7+ hours of sleep, adequate protein (roughly 1.2–1.6g per kg bodyweight daily, so ~85–110g for you), and consistent meals. This matters more than any single workout detail.

Back Pain — Specific Guidance For You
Your back pain pattern (slight, linked to carrying toddlers and long sitting) is extremely common and responds very well to exactly the kind of training in this plan: hip hinge strength (Romanian deadlifts, hip thrusts), anti-extension core work (planks, dead bugs, bird dogs), and glute strengthening. These rebuild the support structure around your spine. A few extra rules:

Always Do

  • Brace your core (like before a light punch to the stomach) before any lift involving your back
  • Keep your lower back's natural curve — neither rounded nor over-arched — during squats and hinges
  • Stop a rep immediately if you feel sharp or shooting pain (different from normal muscle fatigue)

Avoid For Now

  • Heavy barbell deadlifts from the floor until your hip-hinge pattern is confident (the DB Romanian deadlift in this plan builds toward this safely)
  • Loaded spinal flexion exercises like weighted sit-ups or Russian twists with heavy weight
  • Lifting your kids by bending your back — bend your knees and hips instead, same pattern you're training on Mondays
